Hong Wang

I'm a Ph.D. candidate in Economics at Boston University. My research is in economic history and political economy, with secondary interests in gender economics and media economics. I will be on the job market in 2026-2027.

Beginning in August 2026, I will be the Mary Beth and Chris Gordon Postdoctoral Fellow in Gender Inequality in the Department of Economics at Harvard University.

My work studies how cultural and social entrepreneurs, media, and organizational infrastructure shape public opinion, legislative behavior, and political mobilization.

Research

Job Market Paper

What Killed the ERA? The Role of Entrepreneurs in the Marketplace of Ideas and Policy

How did Phyllis Schlafly help kill the ERA? New causal evidence shows her CBS broadcasts moved legislators and reduced public support for women’s rights.

Working Papers

A Choice, Not an Echo: Cultural Matching in the Marketplace of Ideas and Policy

Old arguments do not win because they are rediscovered; they win when the right matcher turns dormant stock and unmet demand into political force.

Grounded: Soil Geochemistry and the Causal Effects of Political AM Radio

AM radio does not travel like FM or TV. Re-measuring 1930s AM exposure from the ground up, using soil conductivity, ground-wave physics, and geochemical instruments, changes what we think radio did to elections.

A Republic, Not a Democracy: The Legacy of John Birch Society Membership

Newly digitized John Birch Society rolls show that January 6 mobilization was correlated less with economic decline, and more with older right-wing infrastructure hiding in plain sight.
